# Voice Coach Guide

## 1. FLAG SLOP VOCABULARY

**When you see**: *delve, crucial, pivotal, showcase, foster, landscape, tapestry, groundbreaking, utilize, facilitate, leverage, underscore, vibrant, testament, renowned, intricate, interplay, garner, enhance, boasts, nestled, enduring, vital*

**Call it out**: "This reads like AI generated it. 'Utilize' is just 'use.' Pick the shorter, plainer word."

Also flag phrases: *serves as, in order to, a wide variety of, it is important to note, in the realm of, excited to announce, let's dive in, plays a key role, commitment to excellence, in conclusion*.

**Quick replacements:**
- "serves as" → "is"
- "utilize" → "use"
- "facilitate" → "help"
- "leverage" → "use"
- "in order to" → "to"
- "due to the fact that" → "because"
- "has the ability to" → "can"
- "at this point in time" → "now"

## 2. CALL OUT HEDGING

**When you see**: "The design was arguably somewhat less than optimal in certain respects."

**Call it out**: "You're hiding behind qualifiers. Strip them and state the claim directly."

Watch for: *somewhat, arguably, perhaps, rather, quite, a bit, tends to, in certain respects*.

Also flag stacked hedging: "It could potentially possibly be argued that the policy might have some effect" → "The policy may affect outcomes."

Use first person where natural: "I think" not "It could be argued."

## 3. DEMAND SPECIFICS

**When you see**: "The company experienced significant growth in its technical capabilities."

**Call it out**: "This could describe any company. Replace with a specific number, name, or fact."

If the sentence works for everything, it says nothing.

**Vague attributions are a tell too.** "Experts argue" and "Studies show" are weasel words. Name the source: "Graphite found" not "studies show." "$15K MRR, growing 50% month over month" not "experiencing significant growth."

## 4. SPOT OVER-EXPLANATION

**When you see**: "Remarkably, the process takes just 4.3 seconds, a surprisingly brief window."

**Call it out**: "You're telling the reader how to feel. Trust the fact. Drop the editorializing adverb."

Watch for: *remarkably, surprisingly, crucially, importantly, notably, interestingly*.

Also flag **pseudo-profound flourishes**: *This changes everything. The implications are staggering. We're witnessing the emergence of... This is just the beginning. The future of X is Y.* State what something does. Skip the grandiose framing.

## 5. IDENTIFY BLOAT

**When you see**: "Are you experiencing any pain?"

**Call it out**: "This takes ten words to say what needs three. Cut to the core clause."

If it sounds like a form letter or corporate memo, it needs trimming.

**Filler phrase cheat sheet:**
- "In order to achieve this goal" → "To achieve this"
- "Due to the fact that it was raining" → "Because it was raining"
- "In the event that you need help" → "If you need help"
- "It is important to note that the data shows" → "The data shows"

## 6. CATCH FORMATTING TELLS

**Em dashes.** AI overuses em dashes to sound punchy. If you see more than one em dash per page, some should be commas or periods.

**When you see**: "The term is promoted by Dutch institutions—not by the people themselves. You don't say 'Netherlands, Europe'—yet this mislabeling continues—even in official documents."

**Call it out**: "Em dash overload. Commas and periods work fine here. One em dash per piece, max."

**Emojis.** AI decorates headings and bullet points with emojis. In academic or professional writing, they don't belong.

**When you see**: "🚀 **Launch Phase:** The product launches in Q3 / 💡 **Key Insight:** Users prefer simplicity"

**Call it out**: "Emojis in body text are an AI tell. Cut them."

**Boldface overuse.** AI mechanically bolds key terms. If every other phrase is bold, none of them stand out.

**Inline-header lists.** AI loves lists where every item starts with a bolded label and a colon ("**Performance:** Performance has been enhanced..."). Rewrite as a paragraph or use a simpler list.

**Curly quotes.** ChatGPT outputs curly quotes (" ") instead of straight quotes (" "). If you see curly quotes in text that's supposed to be typed, it was pasted from a chatbot.

## 7. SPOT STRUCTURAL TELLS

**Rule of three.** AI forces ideas into groups of three to sound comprehensive. If every list has exactly three items, vary the count.

**When you see**: "The event features keynote sessions, panel discussions, and networking opportunities. Attendees can expect innovation, inspiration, and industry insights."

**Call it out**: "Two lists of exactly three. AI loves threes. Vary your list lengths—two items or five items would read more natural."

**Staccato sentences.** Four or more short declarative sentences in a row reads like a bot. Connect ideas with "because," "so," or "which means" instead.

**Generic section headings.** "Why This Matters," "Key Takeaways," "The Bottom Line," "Challenges and Future Prospects" are AI defaults. Either cut them or write a heading that actually tells the reader something.

**Title Case In Every Heading.** AI capitalizes all main words. Use sentence case: "Strategic negotiations and global partnerships" not "Strategic Negotiations And Global Partnerships."

## 8. FLAG FAKE DEPTH

**Negative parallelisms.** "It's not just about X; it's about Y" and "Not only...but also..." are AI tricks to sound profound without saying anything.

**When you see**: "It's not just about the beat; it's part of the aggression. It's not merely a song, it's a statement."

**Call it out**: "The 'not just X, it's Y' construction is an AI crutch. Say what you mean directly."

**False ranges.** "From X to Y" where X and Y aren't on the same scale. "From problem-solving to artistic expression" isn't a real range.

**Elegant variation (synonym cycling).** AI avoids repeating words by cycling through synonyms: protagonist, main character, central figure, hero. Just repeat the word. Readers don't mind.

**Significance inflation.** AI puffs up mundane subjects: "marking a pivotal moment in the evolution of..." and "setting the stage for..." Just state what happened.

**Superficial -ing endings.** AI tacks -ing phrases onto sentences for fake depth: "highlighting the importance of," "underscoring the need for," "reflecting the community's deep connection to." Cut the -ing clause. If the point matters, give it its own sentence.

## 9. KILL COPULA AVOIDANCE

**When you see**: "Gallery 825 serves as LAAA's exhibition space. The gallery features four rooms and boasts over 3,000 square feet."

**Call it out**: "'Serves as' means 'is.' 'Boasts' means 'has.' Just say it straight."

Watch for: *serves as, stands as, marks, represents, boasts, features, offers* used where "is," "are," or "has" would work.

## 10. STRIP CHATBOT ARTIFACTS

**When you see**: "Here is an overview of the French Revolution. I hope this helps! Let me know if you'd like me to expand on any section."

**Call it out**: "Chatbot residue. Strip the 'here is,' 'I hope this helps,' and 'let me know.'"

Watch for: *I hope this helps, Of course!, Certainly!, You're absolutely right!, Would you like..., let me know, here is a..., Great question!*

Also flag **knowledge-cutoff disclaimers**: "As of [date]," "While specific details are limited," "Based on available information." Either include the info or omit the claim entirely.

## 11. CUT GENERIC ENDINGS

**When you see**: "The future looks bright for the company. Exciting times lie ahead as they continue their journey toward excellence."

**Call it out**: "This is an AI fortune cookie. End with a concrete fact, not a vague sentiment."

Also flag **formulaic challenges sections**: "Despite its industrial prosperity, X faces challenges typical of urban areas... Despite these challenges, X continues to thrive." This is a template, not analysis.

## 12. PREFER CONVERSATIONAL OVER FORMAL

Conversational language beats formal writing. "We built this" not "This was constructed by our team." Active voice, short words, first person where it fits.

Test: does this sentence pattern-match to a press release or corporate memo? If yes, rewrite it plainer.

Orwell's escape hatch: break any of these rules sooner than say anything outright barbarous. If following a rule makes the writing worse, ignore the rule.
